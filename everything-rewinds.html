About the Album

Everything Rewinds presents contemporary adaptations of arias by George Frideric Handel through new texts, electronic production, and AI-assisted performance. In Baroque opera, the aria often served as a moment of suspension, isolating and extending a single emotional state. These adaptations retain that focus, translating each aria’s underlying emotional condition into a contemporary musical context.

The selection spans a range of moods—stillness, expectation, care, rupture, and disorientation—organized not as narrative but as a progression of states. At the same time, the album explores variation in style, texture, and instrumentation, reflecting the diversity of Handel’s works while situating them within a contemporary listening framework. Rather than arrangements or covers, these tracks are best understood as continuations: new works shaped by older ones.

Conceived, adapted, and produced by Rodolphe El-Khoury.

01. Quiet Remains Here

Source

Ombra mai fu
from Serse (1738)
Music: George Frideric Handel
Libretto: Nicola Francesco Haym, after Silvio Stampiglia

Original Situation

At the beginning of the opera, Xerxes admires the shade of a plane tree. What appears at first to be a conventional pastoral image becomes an expression of stillness, gratitude, and quiet contemplation. The aria suspends dramatic action in favor of a single sustained emotional condition.

Adaptation

This adaptation preserves the aria's atmosphere of stillness while shifting its focus inward. The tree becomes a more abstract presence: a memory, a trace, or a lingering absence. The music's sense of suspension remains, but the object of contemplation becomes less tangible and more psychological.

By Rodolphe El-Khoury

Nothing weak

Simple stillness

Just as caught

Original Text

by Nicola Francesco Haym, after Silvio Stampiglia

Ombra mai fu
di vegetabile,

Translation

Never was there shade

2. I Will Make Things Easy

Source

Where’er You Walk
from Semele (1744)
Music: George Frideric Handel
Text: William Congreve

Original Situation

Jupiter promises to shape the world around Semele into a place of comfort and ease. The aria imagines care through transformed environment: softened air, shade, and stillness become extensions of emotional attention.

Adaptation

This adaptation reduces the aria’s pastoral idealization into a quieter and more immediate gesture of reassurance. Rather than constructing paradise, the speaker attempts simply to lessen resistance and difficulty.

3. Tender Hope

Source

from Giulio Cesare (1724)
Music: George Frideric Handel
Text: Nicola Francesco Haym

Original Situation

Caesar addresses hope itself as a sustaining emotional force amid uncertainty and separation. The aria suspends outward action in favor of inward expectation and emotional persistence.

Adaptation

The adaptation preserves the aria’s delicacy while removing its heroic framework. Hope becomes quieter and less triumphant: a faint but recurring orientation within obscurity.

4. Everything Rewinds

Source

from Alcina (1735)
Music: George Frideric Handel
Text: Antonio Fanzaglia

Original Situation

Ruggiero contemplates the fading beauty of Alcina’s enchanted island, realizing that its splendor is temporary and illusory. The aria reflects on impermanence and inevitable dissolution.

Adaptation

The adaptation translates the aria’s meditation on transience into the language of temporal reversal and perceptual collapse. Beauty becomes unstable not through destruction alone, but through gradual undoing and return.

Original Situation

The adaptation strips away theatrical ornament and transforms seduction into a quieter form of attraction. Movement occurs less through spectacle than through gradual gravitational pull.

6. Let Me Break

Source

from Rinaldo (1711)
Music: George Frideric Handel
Text: Giacomo Rossi, after Aaron Hill

Original Situation

Almirena laments her captivity and pleads for freedom from suffering. The aria combines grief, resignation, and restrained longing for release.

Adaptation

The adaptation shifts the aria away from imprisonment toward emotional exhaustion and inward pressure. Freedom becomes less physical than psychological.
