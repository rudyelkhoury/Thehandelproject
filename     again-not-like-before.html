About the Album

Again, Not Like Before is the second album by The Handel Project. While Everything Rewinds focused primarily on Handel’s operatic arias, this album turns toward another body of work: psalm settings, sacred compositions, biblical dramas, and allegorical works, including Dixit Dominus, Theodora, Saul, Belshazzar, Jephtha, La Resurrezione, and Il Trionfo del Tempo e del Disinganno.

The project does not seek to reproduce Handel’s original texts or modernize their narratives. Each adaptation begins by identifying the emotional architecture of the source material and rebuilding it through contemporary images, situations, and gestures capable of carrying the same expressive weight.

Across these adaptations, theology becomes imagery. A stream becomes a moment of renewal. Darkness becomes a withdrawal from the world. A summons becomes the return of what has been hidden. Distance becomes a way of seeing. Open hands receive more than they can hold.

The original stories recede, but their emotional logic remains. Many of the works represented here are less concerned with dramatic action than with moments of reflection, recognition, perseverance, dread, loss, release, gratitude, and altered return. Rather than advancing a story, they pause to inhabit a condition. Many of these songs are organized around acts of recognition: the moment when something long present becomes visible.

The title Again, Not Like Before carries two movements at once. It suggests continuity: these songs continue to sing across time. It also suggests transformation: what returns is never simply restored. Together, those meanings define the album’s central movement, from renewal and concealment toward recognition, release, and quiet awakening.

Conceived, adapted, and produced by Rodolphe El-Khoury.

1. Begin Again

Source

De torrente in via bibet
from Dixit Dominus
Music: George Frideric Handel
Original Character: N/A
Key: C minor

Original Situation

A brief passage from Psalm 110 describes a figure who pauses to drink from a stream during a journey before lifting his head and continuing forward.

Dramatic Context

Dixit Dominus is a psalm setting rather than a dramatic narrative. The verse appears near the conclusion of a larger vision of struggle, endurance, judgment, and divine authority. In the midst of movement and conflict, a brief act of refreshment becomes the source of renewed strength.

Emotional Architecture

effort → renewal → continuation

Adaptation

The biblical imagery is reduced to its emotional motion. The brook becomes a pause in a larger journey. Drinking becomes renewal. The lifting of the head becomes the decision to continue.

2. The Far Side of Night

Source

With Darkness Deep, as Is My Woe
from Theodora
Music: George Frideric Handel
Original Character: Theodora
Key: C minor

Original Situation

Theodora calls upon darkness to conceal her and welcomes death rather than submission and dishonor.

Dramatic Context

Theodora, a Christian convert living under Roman rule, has refused to participate in pagan rites. Facing imprisonment, humiliation, and execution, she withdraws from the visible world. Darkness becomes both refuge and refusal.

Emotional Architecture

doubt → breach → damage → withdrawal

Adaptation

The religious and dramatic framework is removed. The adaptation focuses on the aftermath of a rupture or realization that cannot be reversed. The central gesture is concealment: drawing the curtain and leaving the light elsewhere.

3. Something Waiting

Source

Music: George Frideric Handel
Original Character: Jephtha
Key: G minor

Original Situation

Jephtha foresees the consequences of a vow he has made and imagines the suffering that will follow.

Dramatic Context

Near the end of the oratorio, Jephtha realizes that a vow made in confidence has placed his daughter Iphis in mortal danger. Fear arrives before catastrophe. The aria is not an account of what has happened, but a vision of what may be approaching.

Emotional Architecture

premonition → dread → anticipation → helplessness

Adaptation

The biblical narrative is removed. The adaptation preserves the experience of anticipating an unnamed disaster. Ordinary time becomes charged with warning even though no visible event has yet occurred.

4. Limits

Source

Recall, O King!
from Belshazzar
Music: George Frideric Handel
Original Character: Chorus of Jews
Key: F minor

Original Situation

The captive Jews warn Belshazzar against using sacred vessels from the Temple during a royal feast.

Dramatic Context

Belshazzar orders holy vessels taken from Jerusalem to be used for profane display. The chorus warns him that inherited power does not remove consequence. The episode foreshadows his downfall and centers on the humbling of pride.

Emotional Architecture

expansion → presumption → boundary → belated recognition

Adaptation

The adaptation preserves the theme of hubris while shifting it to a secular register. The king becomes an unnamed figure who continually expands the scope of his plans, convinced that limits can always be postponed. Judgment becomes a quieter realization: time itself cannot be negotiated with.

5. Hidden

Source

Infernal Spirits
from Saul
Music: George Frideric Handel
Original Character: The Witch of Endor
Key: C major

Original Situation

The Witch of Endor invokes supernatural powers to summon the spirit of Samuel on behalf of Saul.

Dramatic Context

Near the end of Saul, the king is isolated and unable to obtain guidance. Samuel is dead, and Saul turns to the Witch of Endor for forbidden counsel. What returns is not consolation, but a truth Saul has long resisted.

Emotional Architecture

absence → disturbance → return → confrontation

Adaptation

The adaptation reimagines the scene as the return of what has been hidden, suppressed, forgotten, or denied. The ritual structure of the original aria is preserved, but the object of invocation becomes psychological and historical rather than supernatural.

6. Hidden Still [Instrumental]

Source

Infernal Spirits
from Saul
Music: George Frideric Handel
Original Character: Instrumental companion to Hidden
Key: C major

Original Situation

This instrumental track follows Hidden and extends its position at the album’s center.

Dramatic Context

The track is connected to the same source material as Hidden, but it does not introduce a separate textual or dramatic reading. Its function is structural: it holds the album at the threshold opened by the preceding track.

Emotional Architecture

suspension → continuation → wordless emergence

Adaptation

Where Hidden translates the emotional architecture of Handel’s aria into contemporary language, Hidden Still performs the same translation using musical means alone. No new text is introduced. Fragments of the harmonic, melodic, and atmospheric material are allowed to unfold without narrative or explanation.

7. Beyond All Measure

Source

Thou, God Most High
from Belshazzar
Music: George Frideric Handel
Original Character: Nitocris
Key: E minor

Original Situation

Nitocris reflects upon the permanence and supremacy of God, whose reign extends through all space and time.

Dramatic Context

Belshazzar recounts the fall of Babylon and the consequences of pride and misplaced confidence. In this aria, Nitocris steps back from immediate events and contemplates a scale against which human greatness diminishes.

Emotional Architecture

vastness → perspective → diminishment → continuation

Adaptation

The theological framework is replaced by the experience of viewing Earth from orbit. Distance alters perception. Cities, coastlines, rivers, mountains, borders, and maps gradually lose definition. The original movement from immensity to humility is preserved through changing scale rather than explicit doctrine.

8. They Come Down

Source

From Celestial Seat Descending
from Hercules
Music: George Frideric Handel
Original Character: Priest of Jupiter
Key: G major

Original Situation

The Priest of Jupiter interprets the descent of a celestial eagle as a sign of divine favor and the power of love.

Dramatic Context

Hercules has returned victorious, and public celebrations are underway. The appearance of the eagle is read as an omen. The aria develops a classical idea: even divine beings are subject to desire.

Emotional Architecture

elevation → descent → surrender → universality

Adaptation

The mythological framework is removed while preserving the argument. Figures who appear elevated above ordinary life, in rooms of glass and light and towers in the air, ultimately descend into the same attachments and vulnerabilities as everyone else.

Original Situation

Dramatic Context

In Samson, the imprisoned hero attributes his ruin to the seductive power of Delilah, whose charms won his trust before delivering betrayal. The aria reflects on the forces that transformed his life.

Adaptation

The adaptation reimagines enchantment on a collective scale. The ‘charm’ becomes the promise of progress: the belief that every boundary can be crossed, every distance reduced, and every limit transformed into opportunity.

11. Again, Not Like Before

Source

Ecco il sol
from La Resurrezione
Music: George Frideric Handel
Original Character: Saint John the Evangelist
Key: A major

Original Situation

Saint John announces the arrival of the sun after darkness and celebrates the triumph of life, light, and renewal.

Dramatic Context

La Resurrezione depicts the events surrounding Christ’s resurrection. In this scene, the rising sun functions as both literal image and symbol of renewal. An ending becomes a beginning.

Emotional Architecture

return → recognition → renewal → transformation

Adaptation

The religious framework and solar imagery are removed while preserving the movement from absence to renewal. The adaptation focuses on recognizing someone close—a friend? a lover?—returning to a familiar room after a long absence. The person and the place are recognizable, but altered. The cycle begins again, but not in the same way.

Original Situation

Dramatic Context

Following his victory over Goliath, David becomes both celebrated and threatened. He receives friendship, protection, opportunity, and guidance while navigating increasingly dangerous circumstances. The aria reflects on generosity that exceeds deserving and exceeds measure.

Emotional Architecture

connection → gift → abundance

Adaptation

The theological framework is replaced by a meditation on kindness, contingency, generosity, and the countless forces that sustain a life. The central word carried forward from the source is not “Lord,” but “numberless.”
